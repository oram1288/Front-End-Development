The Great White

Carcharodon carcharias, mainly known as the Great White Shark, is one of 450 shark species and is the largest shark in the ocean today. Great White Sharks ancestry dates back more than 400 million years ago, this beautiful creature represents one of the oceans primary apex predators. Great white sharks live in mostly all coastal and offshore waters which have water temperature between 12 and 24 °C(54 and 75 °F), with greater populations in the United States, South Africa, Japan and Chile. Great Whites often opt to keep their eyes open while sleeping, because some sharks must swim constantly to keep oxygen-rich water flowing over their gills, it has long been noted that some don't sleep at all. These apex predators get stressed in captivity and eventually die when they’re not in their natural environment. Due to their need to travel long distances for seasonal migration and their demanding diet, it is not logistically feasible to keep great white sharks in captivity.

Great Whites Size, Diet and Natural Threats

Adult great white sharks can grow to a maximum size of about 20 feet in length, weigh up to 6,600 pounds, and are estimated to live for 30 years. Deep Blue is a female great white shark that is estimated to be 6.1 m (20 ft) long or bigger and is now 60 years old. She is believed to be one of the biggest ever recorded in history. The shark was first spotted in Mexico by researcher Mauricio Hoyos Padilla.

The great white shark has a diverse diet of fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals. Young white sharks mainly eat bottom fish, smaller sharks and rays, and schooling fish and squids. Larger white sharks often gather around seal and sea lion colonies to feed and also occasionally scavenge dead whales. A Great White can devour a whole seal and wouldn't need to eat another meal for up to three months after that.

Great whites only have one recorded natural predator, the orca. Orcas are the real apex predators of the ocean, they may be the only animals that actually prey on great white sharks. The species also faces numerous challenges which has resulted in international protection. Sharks are hunted for their fins and teeth and are often caught in the nets of commercial and illegal fishermen.

Behaviour and Relationships with Humans/Attacks

Although these sharks are often classified as loners, there is a degree of social hierarchy that does exist among them. Female sharks often dominate males.

While great whites are one of the few species known to have bitten and killed people, these events are extremely rare. Typically, when a white shark does bite a person, it only takes one exploratory bite and quickly realizes that the person is not its preferred prey.

White sharks are among the top three shark species most likely to injure humans, alongside tiger sharks and bull sharks. But only 57 shark bites were recorded in 2022, and that's across all shark species. There are far more survivors of white shark bites than there are fatalities.

Great White Sharks rarely resort to combat with each other and humans. Even though shark attacks have occurred, research has shown that sharks are generally picky eaters and are not inclined to prey on humans.

The great white shark is also known as the man-eating shark, from the the novel Jaws by Peter Benchley and the movie adaptation by Steven Spielberg.



Some Interesting Facts:

  • Sharks do not have bones.

  • Most sharks have good eyesight, though they lack color vision.

  • Sharks have special electroreceptor organs.

  • Shark skin feels similar to sandpaper.

  • Scientists age sharks by counting the rings on their vertebrae.

  • Great White Sharks existed before Dinosaurs.

  • Great White Sharks are actually conflict-avoidant.

  • Great Whites are industrious and efficient.
